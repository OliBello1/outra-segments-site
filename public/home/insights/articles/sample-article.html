The household is the new cookie

Why UK addressability is consolidating around household-level identity — and what that means for the next twelve months of media planning.

April 2025 · 4 min read

For most of the past decade UK media planning relied on the cookie as the unit of addressability. The cookie was a stand-in for a person, sort of, in a browser, on a device. We all knew it was leaky. We all knew it was about to die. And yet the entire DSP, DMP and attribution stack was built around it.

The cookie's replacement isn't another cookie. It isn't a probabilistic graph either. It's the household — the actual physical unit where most consumer decisions are made.

Three forces

First, addressable TV. Sky AdSmart and ITVx ship household-level frequency capping by default. CTV inventory now exceeds 75% reach.

Second, retail media. Tesco, Sainsbury's, Boots — every major UK retailer has a media network keyed on household, not on cookies.

Third, regulatory clarity. The ICO has consistently signalled that household-level addressability — with the right governance — is privacy-acceptable.

What this means

Plan at household. Buy at household. Measure at household. Then translate to the channel-specific identifier at the activation moment, not before.

Practically: stop modelling lookalikes on Meta. Start modelling them on a stable household graph and pushing the matched output to Meta.

The risk

The household identifier becomes the next walled garden if a single owner controls it. The UK answer to this has been federated identity graphs (Outra is one) — multiple operators, audited methodology, ICO oversight.
